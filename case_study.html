Collaborative Design Project: User Experience Studio

Context

One of my classes this semester focused on a group based project, responding to one of two design briefs: care or ceremony. We were to work in groups of three (we got lucky and got a fourth!) to iteratively research, plan and design a product around:

"Care is a fundamental aspect of human and non-human life, encompassing emotional, physical, and social interactions. It includes self-care, care for others, caregiving services, and broader societal structures that support well-being"

"Ceremony is a unified ritualistic event with a purpose. It is how people mark moments as meaningful through intentional, repeated, and shared actions, involving objects, space, and sequence. Ceremonies can be formal or informal, traditional or contemporary, collective or personal."

Whichever brief we chose, we had to create a mid-fidelity prototype and conduct user testing through a testing fair (my first ever!)

Requirements

Must be a physical product or experience

Must include some digital aspect

Must integrate either spatial, processual or experiental elements

Our first step was figuring out a problem space to tackle. What area did we end up exploring?

Intent

Children. Beloved, technologically savvy little gremlins who seem these days to love nothing more than staring at an ipad all day, every day.

Many studies in the last few years have demonstrated the many impacts a largely technology based upbringing has on the cognitive development and creative thinking in children, and we wanted to tackle that growing gap.

We did not ever want to fully replace technology for children;

It wouldn't work! Kids who have screens want to keep them

Our product needs digital features, so it would be hypocritical to say it's replacing technology (when it is technology)

Our goal was to create a product that encouraged creative thinking in children, and was a productive, entertaining alternative activity for screentime.

The Process

Research

Our first step was user research - to determine if our problem space was indeed, actually a problem happening at scale.

Across the group we completed scientific research, interviews, digital collages and an online ethnography. Every member contributed to the scientific research, and I was responsible for the online ethnography.

Scientific research gives our problem credibility and metrics.

Interviews allow for rich qualitative data and getting answers to specific questions.

Digital Collages are a fun way to communicate ideas with children, and reveal emotional and thematic connections between elements.

An online ethnography reveals if the problem is felt 'on the ground', and the way it impacts real people across large demographics.

Online Ethnography

This ethnography focused on 'excessive amounts of screentime in children', particularly in regards to physical, emotional and cognitive skills. I chose Reddit as my source because it hosts a wealth of information in a very active community, and the forum structure makes organisation and specification of questions and answers very easy. I gathered roughly 117 responses from 10 subreddits to form the ethnography.

This is the thematic analysis born from the online ethnography. I use 'thematic' slightly more loosely here than perhaps traditional; basing groupings of off 'interpretation' instead of over arching themes. I chose this because for me, it offers more nuance and the ability to group together similar sentiments whilst maintaining the specificity of the original responses.

Storyboards and Sketches

Every group member created 3 storyboards of potential solutions, which were then critiqued by each other and our tutor. After discussions, pulling out what we liked about each idea and what other directions we could move in, we decided on an initial concept - a pencil case with a projector.

We did not keep this initial concept.

We liked a lot about the idea, however ultimately felt it was too similar to products already on the market, and we could not find a way to add the projector that would be physically possible in any capacity.

Proper Prototypes

After discussion and approval from our tutor (along with one suggestion regarding increasing the size more), we moved into constructing our final prototype.

I was in charge of creating the physical components, and my groupmates were in charge of creating the screens.

Quick Screen Appreciation

Our screens were prototyping two user flows (and our two main functions):

The 'free play' mode, which housed a gallery of templates for children to choose from and project at their own pace,

And the 'story' mode, which consists of a game in which children must help 'Woofles' (our titular mascot) in and out of various situations using the magic stylus to complete harder and harder templates.



Physical Prototype!

I constructed our physical prototype out of paper mache - it seemed to be the most appropriate option for the curved shape we were looking for.

Even though I did scale up again from our low-fi prototypes, after building the first body and test-fitting my phone screen inside, I realised it was still too small. I built a second, much larger version as our final.

The main change I had to make from low to mid-fi prototype was the screen; the only option we had for the screen was to use a phone inside, and the curved shape of the body meant the phone was internally offset too significantly. After communication with the group, we decided to inset the screen face to allow the phone to press flat.


I used my smaller model as a guinea pig to test how to inset the screen without remaking the entire body, which we did not have time for.

After insetting the face in the final body, the slits were covered in more paper to reinforce the newly weakened stress points, and to give it a little bit more polish.

We originally wanted four arrow buttons to mimic a game console, however it could not be positioned correctly while maintaining the size and proportion of the body and the buttons - that need to be large to accomodate clumsy fingers.

After discussion, I went with only two arrows, left and right, alongside the central 'confirm' button. The proportional balance and spacing between components was better, and we felt the tradeoff of convenience would not be a large enough source of friction to require the vertical arrows.

User Test Results

Our user test results were overall quite positive! We used a mix of interviews, think-alouds, and an SUS survey;

Think alouds reveal which touch points and interactions work, and where - but most importantly why - user confusion is happening.

Interviews help gather findings about the project outside of immediate thoughts, and in our case, help bridge the thinking of young adult testers, and our young target audience.

SUS surveys give quantitative data about the quality of the project, and paired with think-aloud answering, key in design gaps and success.

Impact: How to measure theoretical success?

We can't measure success in the traditional sense - analytics, number improvements, units sold. Ultimately, our student project stopped at mid fidelity, let alone shipping to real customers and being able to track quantitative data.

We can reflect on our user testing results to gauge preliminary impact: with an average SUS score of 82, our design was solid.

Interview results were positive, with quotes such as

"If i was a kid and i was bored, i would just hop on it"

"It was very engaging in terms of aesthetic, which is a big part in how people perceive things"

"Honestly I would use it all the time. Even at school on breaks at recess"

Our biggest learning was that many users needed assistance on the very first interaction with the prototype, asking for clarity in navigation.

"For a kid..., you need to teach them the process, but other than that it would be clear"

However, many believed that once taught, no one would struggle with remembering the functions, meaning if we provided written instructions with a shipped product, this friction would likely be mitigated.

With that being said,

how would we measure our impact, if we could?

In an ideal world, I would like to run a pilot test - select a group of children, measure their active screen time and (subjective) ability to think creatively, send the product off and document changes in those metrics.

Potentially, even looking at schools who educate children who use the product, and document grade changes and/or class engagement over time.

More realistically there would be more of a reliance on sales metrics and user reviews to guide the scale of impact we would theoretically achieve.

Reflecting on Our Process

What Went Well!

Our communication as a group was amazing. Every step of the way, whether it was inherently collaborative or independent, there was an abundance of clear, productive communication between members. Asking questions and hearing everyone's perspective was very important for us, and no one was afraid to challenge or seek clarification throughout our design journey.

What I Would Change

Changes I would make live mostly in our user testing. It was the first time we were following our particular format, and we didn't run a test on each other before the fair to iron out any wrinkles in our questions and methods.

There were quite a few wrinkles. Nothing terrible, but our first participant ended up being our guinea pig - which meant we didn't get the best information we could have.

What Did I Learn?

Firstly, to trust your team. I didn't fully believe in the vision the others had for the final design, and ideated to myself on the side. I never shared the idea, but it was a lot less creative than the direction we went, and my group did a wonderful job answering all of my technical concerns. I should have trusted them from the beginning and saved myself the headache of stressing about alternative plans.

These were my alternate designs. Same function, different form. A boring form. Safe.

Pairing with what went well, communication is so so important to the success of the project! Knowing who is doing what, being able to match strengths to tasks, and being able to trust that everyone would ask for clarification if they needed it removed so much potential stress and confusion. Everyone knew what they were doing, why they were doing it, and when things needed to be done.

Overall,

This was a really fun project to work on collaboratively, and although there were definitely some hiccups, this is by far the design process that has taught me the most - not only about product design, but also trusting the process, testing that process beforehand, and how successful communication is the backbone of a successful project. This truly would not have been possible without my wonderful groupmates, and my tutor - who was equally supportive and critical. That experienced perspective kept us in check and on course, without stifling our creativity.
